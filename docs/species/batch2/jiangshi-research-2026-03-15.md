## 1. Folklore Summary

The Jiangshi (僵屍, "stiff corpse"; sometimes romanized as Chiang-shih or geungsi in Cantonese) is a reanimated cadaver from Chinese folk belief whose defining characteristic is the pathological rigidity of its body. Unlike the fluid, seductive vampires of European tradition or the shambling undead of modern popular culture, the Jiangshi moves by hopping — both feet together, arms extended forward — because rigor mortis has locked its limbs. This distinctive locomotion, which appears superficially comic to outside observers, is in the source tradition a marker of profound cosmological disorder: a body so thoroughly abandoned by its animating spirit (*hun*, 魂) that the coarser somatic spirit (*po*, 魄) has become trapped in corrupted flesh, animating it in ways that defy natural order. The Jiangshi is not precisely a "vampire" in the Western sense, though it feeds on the living; it absorbs *qi* (氣, vital breath or life-force) from its victims, typically through proximity and breath, rather than through blood-drinking — a distinction that carries significant theological weight in the Chinese cosmological framework within which the legend emerged (Groot, 1892–1910, vol. 4, pp. 71–82).

The circumstances that produce a Jiangshi are multiple and are all rooted in failures of proper death ritual or cosmologically disordered dying. Primary causes documented across regional sources include: failure to conduct adequate burial rites (*bìnzàng*, 殯葬) — particularly relevant for those who die away from home and whose bodies cannot be quickly returned for proper interment; violent death (suicide, murder, battlefield death) that creates an unresolved grievance preventing the hun from departing; a corpse struck by lightning before burial; a corpse over which a cat leaps (*māo tiào shī*, 貓跳屍), causing galvanic reanimation in some traditions; or prolonged exposure to intense moonlight. In each case the underlying logic is consistent: the hun has departed incompletely or violently, leaving the po stranded in a body with residual qi that cannot dissipate. The Jiangshi is the result of this entrapped, corrupted animating energy continuing to seek vitality from external sources (Kang, 2006, pp. 39–52).

The Jiangshi as popularly depicted — and as standardized substantially through Hong Kong cinema from the 1970s through 1990s — wears Qing dynasty (1644–1912) official robes (*guānfú*, 官服) with a yellow paper talisman (*fúlù*, 符籙) affixed to its forehead. This aesthetic is largely a late folkloric and cinematic consolidation rather than an ancient standard: pre-Qing sources describe the Jiangshi in more varied dress corresponding to burial attire of the period. The Qing-robes convention appears to have crystallized during the Republican era and reflects the cultural distance Republican-era audiences felt from Qing imperial culture — the Qing robes made the Jiangshi visually "other," marked as belonging to a rejected political past. Scholars of Chinese religion note that this aesthetic should be understood as a historically contingent visual shorthand rather than a timeless cosmological fact (Kang, 2006, pp. 58–61). The yellow talisman, by contrast, is theologically grounded: Mao Shan (茅山) Taoist priests, who specialize in the command and control of spirits and reanimated corpses, use precisely these written talismans (*fúlù*) — inscribed with commands in the name of specific Taoist divine authorities — to immobilize or redirect Jiangshi. The Mao Shan tradition, based in Jiangsu Province and traceable to the 4th-century Taoist master Tao Hongjing, is the canonical source of Jiangshi exorcism and control techniques within Chinese religious practice.

The most anthropologically distinctive Jiangshi tradition is the practice of the *ganshi ren* (趕屍人, "corpse driver" or "corpse herder"), documented primarily from Hunan Province. Given the importance in Chinese folk religion of dying and being buried in one's ancestral village, families who had a member die far from home — soldiers, migrants, merchants — faced the logistical and ritual problem of returning the corpse across difficult terrain. The *ganshi ren* was a specialist, operating within a Taoist framework, who would use ritual techniques to cause the corpses of several deceased individuals to walk or hop — guided by small bells and a paper lantern — along mountain roads back to their home villages, typically traveling only at night. This practice, attested in Hunan provincial records and ethnographic accounts from the late Qing and Republican periods, may reflect actual historical practices involving strapping corpses to bamboo poles carried by bearers, rendered through a ritual-magical interpretive frame (Groot, 1892–1910; Kang, 2006, pp. 45–48). The *ganshi ren* tradition establishes a key distinction absent in Western vampire lore: in Chinese tradition, the reanimated corpse is not necessarily a free-agent predator. It can be controlled, directed, and put to a (grim) practical use by a sufficiently skilled ritual specialist. This controllability is theologically central — it reflects the Chinese religious conception of the supernatural world as a bureaucratic hierarchy that can be navigated by those with the right credentials and texts.

## 3. Sensitivity Review

**Sensitivity Level:** MED

The Jiangshi sits in an intermediate zone of sensitivity. Chinese folk religion, from which it emerges, is not an extinct tradition — it remains actively practiced across mainland China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and diaspora communities, and the Mao Shan Taoist tradition that provides the canonical Jiangshi exorcism framework is a living lineage with ordained practitioners. The Jiangshi itself is not a sacred figure within that tradition (it represents disorder, not reverence), but the Mao Shan exorcism methods, talisman traditions, and the role of the Taoist priest should be depicted with care and specificity rather than reduced to generic "Chinese magic." The greater sensitivity risk, however, is aesthetic: the visual shorthand of Qing-dynasty robes risks flattening Chinese cultural specificity into a single dynastic period's aesthetics, which could read as reducing "Chinese" to a single historical moment. This is a costume-design and art-direction concern as much as a content concern.

**Recommendations:**
- Be specific about the Mao Shan Taoist lineage when depicting exorcist NPCs — generic "Taoist priest" without lineage specificity flattens a diverse tradition
- Do not use Qing-dynasty robes as the default Jiangshi aesthetic without acknowledging this is a late cinematic convention; consider offering period-appropriate burial attire variants
- The *ganshi ren* corpse-driving tradition is rich and unusual enough to warrant careful, respectful implementation — it should not be played for horror comedy
- Avoid conflating Jiangshi with Western vampire tropes (blood-drinking, seduction, aristocratic aesthetics) — the qi-absorption and breath-detection mechanics are the culturally specific and gameplay-interesting elements

## 4. Cross-Game Applicability

### Precursors (Primary)
- **Archetype Seed:** `controllable_undead` or `qi_predator`
- **Ecological Role:** `environmental_hazard` — Jiangshi as a consequence of improper death mechanics; their presence indicates a burial-rite failure in the simulation
- **Gameplay potential:** Jiangshi spawn as a consequence of improper burial events, battlefield deaths without subsequent rite-of-passage actions, or cats crossing unburied corpses. Breath-detection mechanic creates unique stealth gameplay — players who hold a button to stop their character's visible breathing become invisible to the Jiangshi. Mao Shan priests (NPC specialists) can be hired to immobilize or redirect Jiangshi; glutinous rice creates traversable barriers. The *ganshi ren* subtype could enable a questline in which a specialist NPC moves Jiangshi along fixed routes as a labor/resource system.

  - `social/controllability`: 0.75 (can be commanded by ritual specialists — key distinction from autonomous undead)
  - `reproductive/contagion_reanimation`: 0.60 (incidental reproduction through qi-drain; drives secondary outbreak events)

### MVEE (Secondary)
- **MythGenerationSystem:** Template for "unresolved death" myth archetype — communities generate stories about the walking dead as a direct consequence of inadequate funerary infrastructure or violent social disruption; generalizes to any setting where death-ritual systems can fail
- **RitualSystem:** Burial rites as active game systems whose failure has measurable supernatural consequences; talisman-writing as a specialist skill with theological specificity; glutinous-rice barrier rituals; rooster-keeping as a passive apotropaic practice
- **BeliefGenerationSystem:** Belief that the manner of death determines the fate of the body and soul; belief in qi as a tangible substance that can be absorbed, depleted, and restored; belief in a cosmological bureaucracy of spirit-control that skilled specialists can access
- **SchismSystem:** Potential schism between Mao Shan Taoist specialists (who insist on lineage-specific talisman techniques) and local folk practitioners (who use rice, mirrors, and roosters without formal Taoist training) — competing claims to legitimate anti-Jiangshi authority; further schism between communities that see Jiangshi as a sign of moral/ritual failure vs. those who see them as a practical problem to be managed
- **SyncretismSystem:** In diaspora or contact-zone settings, Jiangshi belief can syncretize with Buddhist *preta* (hungry ghost) traditions; with animist beliefs in spirit-possession; or in modernizing contexts, reinterpreted as a metaphor for improper ancestor veneration, generating a secular-traditional split in community belief about what "corpse-walking" events actually mean
