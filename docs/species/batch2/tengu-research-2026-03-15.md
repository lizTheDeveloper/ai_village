## 1. Folklore Summary

The Tengu (天狗, literally "heavenly dog," though the etymology is contested — the characters were borrowed from the Chinese *tiāngǒu*, a meteor-beast, but the Japanese creature bears little resemblance) are among the most complex and morphologically varied supernatural beings in the Japanese tradition. The earliest textual record appears in the *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE), which describes a large shooting star that Buddhist monks identify as a "heavenly dog" — an omen of war. This initial association with disaster and the disruption of social order characterizes the earliest stratum of Tengu belief: they were agents of chaos, instigators of conflict, and kidnappers of children and monks. By the Heian period (794–1185), literary sources such as the *Konjaku Monogatari-shū* (ca. 1120 CE) depict Tengu as proud, dangerous spirits capable of possessing Buddhist clergy and causing political upheaval. The *Taiheiki* (14th century) identifies deceased warriors and corrupt monks as becoming Tengu after death, a belief that fused the spirit with concepts of karmic consequence and the dangers of overweening pride (*mana*, or arrogance in religious practice) (Reider, 2010).

A crucial transformation occurs across the medieval period, by which Tengu shift from agents of chaos to martial exemplars and, ultimately, protective mountain deities. This transition is most vividly illustrated in the legend of Minamoto no Yoshitsune (1159–1189), the tragic military genius of the Genpei War. According to traditions recorded in the *Gikeiki* (a 14th-century war chronicle), Yoshitsune was spirited away as a boy to the Kurama mountains north of Kyoto, where the great Tengu Sōjōbō taught him the arts of swordsmanship, strategy, and movement with preternatural speed. Sōjōbō is depicted as the king of all Tengu — an elder with an extraordinarily long nose, white hair, white wings, and carrying a *hauchiwa* (feather fan) of tremendous power. This narrative seeded a lasting cultural association between Tengu and the transmission of martial knowledge outside conventional social hierarchies: the Tengu teaches the outcast boy, bypassing the samurai class structure entirely. By the Edo period (1603–1868), Tengu had been incorporated into many *ryū* (martial arts lineages) as mythological founders or transmitters of secret techniques (Draeger, 1973).

The morphological distinction between two Tengu types is taxonomically important and reflects a stratified tradition. The *Dai-Tengu* (大天狗, "great Tengu") — also called *Konoha Tengu* or *Yamabushi Tengu* — are the noble, humanoid form: tall figures with a long, prominent nose (*hanadaka*), red face, wings (often depicted as eagle or hawk feathers), white hair or beard, and carrying a feather fan. They wear the distinctive robes of the *yamabushi* (mountain ascetics), including the small black *tokin* cap, pompom-decorated vest (*suzukake*), and ceremonial staff (*shakujō*). The *Ko-Tengu* (小天狗) or *Karasu Tengu* (烏天狗, "crow Tengu") are fully bird-headed: the face of a crow or bird of prey on a humanoid body. Ko-Tengu function as messengers and retainers in Tengu hierarchies, less powerful and more feral than their great counterparts. Folklorist Noriko Reider (2010) and anthropologist Carmen Blacker (1975) both emphasize that the Dai-Tengu represents the syncretic fusion of indigenous mountain spirit belief (*yama no kami*) with the Buddhist *yamabushi* tradition, while the Ko-Tengu preserves an older, animistic avian-spirit layer.

Tengu inhabit specific named mountains, and many significant peaks in Japan — Kurama, Atago, Hiei, Takao, Daisen — have associated Tengu with personal identities, names, and cult followings. The relationship between mountain, Tengu, and local community was one of cautious negotiation: Tengu would punish those who entered the mountain without proper observance, who cut trees without ritual permission, or who exhibited the pride (*okori*) that Tengu are mythologically constituted to despise. The phenomenon of *Tengu-kakushi* ("hidden by Tengu") denoted mysterious disappearances — people, especially children and monks, who vanished and returned days or weeks later in a state of dissociation, unable to account for lost time. Returned victims often exhibited unusual knowledge, strange visions, or the ability to perform skills they had not previously learned — suggesting that abduction narratives carried a secondary function as explanations for anomalous competence (Blacker, 1975, pp. 179–182). The related phenomenon of *Tengu-tsuki* (Tengu possession) caused victims to speak in strange voices, perform feats of physical strength, and deliver pronouncements — behaviors that in some documented cases were managed by local religious specialists.

## 3. Sensitivity Review

**Sensitivity Level:** MED

Tengu occupy a genuinely complex position in contemporary Japanese religious and cultural life. Several Tengu — most notably Sōjōbō of Kurama and the Tengu of Atago-san — are venerated at active Shinto shrines and mountain religious sites as *kami* or protective spirits, with festivals, statuary, and living devotional practice. The mountain religious tradition of *Shugendo*, which syncretically merged Shinto and Buddhist elements, treats Tengu as spiritually powerful beings that must be respected rather than merely feared. Depicting Tengu as straightforward monsters or villains risks misrepresenting their role in traditions that remain active in Japan. At the same time, Tengu are pervasive in mainstream Japanese media (manga, anime, video games) without significant community objection, and the folklore tradition itself documents Tengu as adversarial, dangerous, and morally complex — the "punisher of the proud" characterization is intrinsic, not an external imposition.

**Recommendations:**
- Preserve the Tengu's moral complexity: they are not evil, but they are proud, dangerous, and governed by a strict code that punishes hypocrisy — this is their defining characteristic and should be central to any implementation
- Avoid flattening Dai-Tengu and Ko-Tengu into a single type; the distinction is culturally meaningful and offers useful gameplay differentiation (noble/intelligent vs. feral/animalistic)
- If Tengu appear as venerable or knowledgeable figures (martial teachers, mountain guardians), this aligns with the tradition's own trajectory and is unlikely to cause offense
- Depictions that mock Shinto mountain worship, use Tengu iconography (tokin cap, hauchiwa) in a degrading context, or present them as mindless demons should be avoided; consider consulting Japanese cultural sensitivity advisors before finalizing character designs
