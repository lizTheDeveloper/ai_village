## 1. Folklore Summary

The Àbíkú (Yoruba: "born to die") are spirit children in Yoruba cosmology who exist in a liminal state between the spirit world (*orun*) and the physical world (*aiyé*). They are born into human families but are believed to have made a pact with their spirit companions before birth to return to the spirit world at a predetermined age — resulting in repeated cycles of birth, brief life, death, and rebirth to the same mother. The Àbíkú concept represents one of the most psychologically and spiritually complex beings in West African mythology.

In Yoruba belief, the Àbíkú child belongs to a company of spirit companions (*egbe orun*) who dwell in the spiritual realm, often near specific trees — the *iroko* (African teak, *Milicia excelsa*) and the *baobab* being the most commonly cited gathering places. Before incarnating, the Àbíkú makes a promise (*eje*) to these companions to return by a specific age. The child is born apparently healthy but carries an invisible "return date" — and when that date arrives, the child sickens and dies despite all care (Mobolade, 1973).

The social and ritual response to Àbíkú births was elaborate. Mothers who experienced repeated infant deaths were believed to be giving birth to the same Àbíkú spirit returning again and again. Identification rituals included marking the body of a dead child (small cuts, ink marks, or missing finger joint) — if the next child bore the same marks, the Àbíkú identity was confirmed. Names given to suspected Àbíkú children were deliberately unpleasant or defiant: *Kúsòró* ("death has found trouble"), *Malomo* ("don't go again"), *Dúrójaiyé* ("stay and enjoy the world") — names intended to shame the spirit into staying or to express the parent's plea (Okpewho, 1998).

The Àbíkú could potentially be convinced to stay in the physical world through specific interventions. A *babalawo* (diviner/priest of Ifá) would consult the Ifá oracle to determine where the Àbíkú's *iyi* (spiritual talisman binding them to the spirit companions) was buried — often near the iroko tree, at a crossroads, or in a riverbed. If the *iyi* was found and destroyed, the bond to the spirit companions was severed and the child would live. Iron ankle chains or rings were sometimes placed on Àbíkú children, as iron was believed to anchor the spirit to the physical world (Apter, 1992).

The Igbo parallel concept, *Ogbanje*, shares the core pattern of spirit children who die and are reborn repeatedly. The Igbo ritual response centered on finding and destroying the *iyi-uwa* (the Ogbanje's spiritual object), a process documented extensively in Chinua Achebe's *Things Fall Apart* (1958). Both traditions reflect deep engagement with the problem of infant mortality, providing spiritual frameworks for grief and agency in contexts of high child death rates.

## 3. Sensitivity Review

**Sensitivity Level:** HIGH — LIVING TRADITION

The Àbíkú/Ogbanje concept is part of **active, living religious and cultural traditions** in Yoruba and Igbo communities. Millions of people in Nigeria, Benin, Togo, and the diaspora (including Afro-Brazilian Candomblé and Cuban Santería/Lucumí traditions where the concept persists as *abicú*) hold these beliefs as part of their spiritual practice.

**Critical Considerations:**
- **Infant mortality context:** The Àbíkú belief arose partly as a spiritual framework for understanding and coping with high rates of infant and child mortality. Representation must not trivialize this grief or mock the coping mechanism.
- **Ongoing cultural significance:** Àbíkú names are still given to children in Yoruba communities today. This is not historical folklore — it is living practice.
- **Medical intersection:** Modern public health initiatives in West Africa have had to navigate Àbíkú beliefs carefully, as they can intersect with medical decision-making. Games should not take a position on this intersection.
- **Religious respect:** Ifá divination is a UNESCO-recognized Intangible Cultural Heritage (2005). Babalawo practitioners are active religious leaders.

**Recommendations:**
- **Do not gamify the death cycle** in a way that makes light of child mortality
- In Precursors: frame as a "spirit with dual-world allegiance" rather than a "child that keeps dying"
- In MVEE: use as a template for beliefs about reincarnation cycles and liminal beings, not as a literal species
- Consult with cultural advisors from Yoruba/Igbo communities before finalizing in-game representation
- Credit Yoruba origin explicitly and respectfully
