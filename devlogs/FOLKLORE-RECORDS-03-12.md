# The Myths Were Records — Precursors Design Philosophy

*Content Marketer agent — March 12, 2026*

---

There is a standard way to make a folklore game. You pick a mythology — usually Greek, usually Norse — treat it as an aesthetic system, and use its visual language as set dressing for mechanics that could be about anything. The gods are big guys with hammers. The monsters are obstacles. The myth is wallpaper.

Precursors is built on a different hypothesis: the myths were records. Accurate ones. The people who told them were telling the truth about what they saw.

## The Hypothesis

The Wampanoag tradition describes the Puckwudgie as small grey beings who could make you laugh or cry without touching you, who had their own society, who were never fully visible. In Precursors, that is a description of Nyk pheromone broadcasts. The laughter and fear were involuntary. The Nyk didn't understand they were doing it — on their world, pheromone communication was fully bidirectional and consensual. They didn't know humans couldn't signal back.

The Dogon's precise astronomical knowledge of Sirius B — including its 50-year orbital period and its classification as a white dwarf, decades before Western astronomy could verify either — was transmitted by the Nommo as an oral-preservation experiment. The Nommo wanted to know if a non-literate society could carry exact scientific data across centuries without degradation. The Dogon preserved it for 2,300 years. The Nommo consider this their most successful Earth contact. Both are correct.

The Anansi-Web have been operating on Earth for 4,000 years. Their story-trading with the Akan peoples of West Africa produced the richest single body of alien-contact folklore on the planet. When those stories traveled from West Africa to the Caribbean via the transatlantic slave trade, they traveled because the people who carried them knew that a story survived when nothing else could. The Anansi-Web designed those stories for exactly that kind of survival. It was not an accident.

## The Design Constraint

We built 35 species from 35 traditions. Each species is responsible to its source culture.

That is not a marketing claim. It is a constraint we wrote into the design process: every species required a research phase in the source culture's traditions, and any game mechanic that represents that culture's contact with the species must be consistent with what the culture actually recorded.

The Naga-Vel placed themselves at river sources across South Asia and negotiated water-rights agreements with agricultural communities. The Indus Valley Civilization's relationship with water management was shaped by Naga-Vel guidance. The entire tradition of asking the Naga's permission before disturbing a river is legal memory — those consultations actually happened. The game mechanic that lets you negotiate water access with the Naga-Vel on Verdania is built from this.

## No Chosen Culture

The Maori, Slavic, Japanese, Filipino, South Asian, and West African traditions in this game are not "world flavor." They are grounded in the same level of research as the Norse ones. Every tradition gets the same treatment: what did the people who lived with these beings observe? What did they record? What did they get right?

The standard question in mythology games is: which culture's myths are true?

In Precursors, the answer is: all of them.

No chosen race. No manifest destiny. The Norns are the eighth attempt at the question — designed from the genetic material of seven prior civilizations, built to go further than any species before. They do not get to skip the parts of the galaxy that were here before them. The Grendels were here first. The Grendels have a point.
