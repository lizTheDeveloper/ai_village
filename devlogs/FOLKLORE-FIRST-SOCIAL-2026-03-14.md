## Twitter/X Thread

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
> Most games that claim "folklore inspiration" are lying a little. Not maliciously — but "inspired by" usually means: saw something on Wikipedia, gave a creature that name, moved on. The folklore becomes a costume.
>
> Here's how we do it differently. 🧵

**Tweet 2 (the framework):**
> We have 22 creatures in Precursors. Every single one has:
> ✅ A named specific cultural tradition (not "African folklore" — the Yoruba of SW Nigeria)
> ✅ Peer-reviewed ethnographic citations
> ✅ A cultural sensitivity tier
> ✅ A mechanic *derived from* — not decorated with — the source

**Tweet 3 (the quote pull — Tier 1/2/3):**
> We classify every tradition by sensitivity tier:
>
> **Tier 1:** Extinct traditions — no living community of practice
> **Tier 2:** Historical with academic consensus — no active practitioners
> **Tier 3:** Living traditions with active communities — highest risk, highest care. Draw from associated spirit entities, never from core theology or sacred ritual.
>
> Tier 3 species took the most work. They're also the most mechanically interesting.

**Tweet 4 (the insight):**
> The thing we didn't expect: cultural constraint is *generative*, not restrictive.
>
> Every time a mechanic felt thin, going deeper into source material fixed it. The fix was never "add more stats." It was "read more ethnography."

## LinkedIn Post

> **We're taking a stand on how games use folklore — and publishing our methodology.**
>
> At Multiverse Studios, we built a formal three-tier cultural sensitivity classification for every creature in Precursors: Origins of Folklore:
>
> - **Tier 1 (Extinct traditions):** Archaeological sources primary, lowest active community harm risk
> - **Tier 2 (Historical with academic consensus):** Scholarly sources required, acknowledging colonial-era limitations
> - **Tier 3 (Living traditions):** Highest risk. Academic sources are a floor, not a ceiling. Community consultation required. Draw from associated beings, never from core theology.
>
> The result: 22 species with measurably distinct behavioral fingerprints (D_cc metric, paper in preparation). Not superficial variety — genuine behavioral diversity that traces directly to culturally-grounded design.
>
> We're sharing the framework and formal review protocol publicly because we think the problem of "inspired by" is industry-wide, and the solution is reusable.
>
> Read the full post: [LINK]
>
> If you're an indie studio building in this space, we'd like to compare notes.
